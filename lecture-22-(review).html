Digital certificates and certificate authorities
...

Remember that the point of authentication was to protect a communication from tampering by a third party. However, the challenge is that you don't necessarily know if the other party you're talking to is actually who you intended to talk to; you might just have a secure connection to an attacker, not the service you wanted to talk to.

This is where digital certificates come into play. A certificate authority (CA) verifies the identity of someone wanting a digital certificate, then digitally signs all of the relevant information using the CA's private key. Obviously, trusting individual certificates is a pain, so we typically trust certificate authorities instead; if we can verify that a particular certificate was signed by a trusted CA, the certificate itself is trusted.

The high-level system is as follows:

  • A service requests a certificate from a CA, which generates a signed certificate stored by both the CA and the service.
  • A client requests a secure communication with the service.
  • The service sends back the certificate as a response.
  • The client reaches out to the (trusted) CA to validate the certificate; if successful, this asserts that the domain name on the certificate is the service that's actually being connected to. (That is, if the certificate has example.com and validation passes, you know that you're talking to example.com.)
  • The service begins communicating with the CA.

If a certificate expires, is revoked, or otherwise can't be validated, your browser will usually tell you that the service is unable to prove that it's claiming what the certificate says it is.

Typically, most browsers and operating systems come with a set of trusted CAs built-in. Unrecognized CAs (or self-signed certificates, which naturally comes from a CA that's not recognized) will give you a warning for many services.
