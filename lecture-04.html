A permutation is an ordered sequence of the elements of a finite set , where each element appears exactly once. In a monoalphabetic cipher, we simply assign each input letter of the alphabet to a particular output, such that the key is a permutation of the alphabet.

If the key can be any permutation of the 26 alphabet characters, then there are keys, or greater than keys. That’s pretty big, and you might think this makes it secure - it’s larger than modern DES, and makes a true brute force attack infeasible. But we can apply frequency analysis, since the properties of the plaintext will carry through to the ciphertext. So a countermeasure is to provide multiple substitutes (homophones) for a single letter.

One other thing we can do is look for digrams and trigrams, two- and three-letter combinations respectively. “th“ and “the” are the most common digram/trigram, respectively.

Playfair ciphers
...

The playfair cipher is the best-known multi-letter encryption cipher, in which plaintext digrams are treated as single units and translated into ciphertext digrams. It was based on the use of a 5 x 5 matrix of letters constructed using a keyword, and was invented in 1854 and used throughout WWI and WWII.

Here, we fill in the letters of the keyword (minus duplicates) from left to right and from top to bottom, filling in the remainder of the matrix (left to right, top to bottom) with the remaining letters in alphabetic order.

The playfair cipher is done like so:

  • The plaintext is encrypted two letters at a time; repeating plaintext letters that are in the same pair are separated by a filler letter, like “x”.
  • Two plaintext letters that fall in the same row of the matrix are each replaced by the letter to the right, with the row wrapping circularly as needed.
  • Two plaintext letters that fall in the same column are each replaced by the letter beneath, with column wrapping circularly as needed.
  • Otherwise, each plaintext letter in a pair is replaced by the letter that lies in its own row and the column occupied by the other plaintext. That is to say, you take the letters at the intersection of the row/column of both plaintext letters, taking the intersecting letter in the row of the first plaintext letter and then the intersecting letter in the row of the second plaintext letter.

Polyalphabetic ciphers
...

Polyalphabetic ciphers improve on the simple monoalphabetic technique by using different monoalphabetic substitutions as one proceeds through the plaintext message. That is to say, a particular plaintext will have a different output throughout the course of the message, such that each letter has a particular key (that may be “reused”, since there’s only 26 letters).

All polyalphabetic techniques have the following features in common:

  • a set of related monoalphabetic substitution rules are used
  • a key determines which particular rule is chosen for a given transformation

The Vigenere cipher is the best known and one of the simplest polyalphabetic substitution ciphers. Here, the set of related monoalphabetic substitution rules is a series of caesar ciphers applied to each letter. Each cipher is then denoted by a key letter, which is the ciphertext letter substituting for the plaintext letter.

To encrypt a message, the key is repeated throughout the course of the message (where the message is typically, but not necessarily, longer than the key - the key is usually a real English word, but you can see how this can become a one-time pad).

We also briefly mention the Vernam cipher, a cipher in which each byte of the plaintext is XOR’d with a byte generated by a key stream generator, which may simply be a keyword. So instead of applying the caesar cipher as the key repeats, we XOR the bytes.

Stream ciphers
...

So far, we’ve looked at substitution ciphers.

Stream ciphers encrypt bits individually (one bit at a time), and are typically fast, leading to their adoption in embedded devices. Block ciphers encrypt several bits at a time - that is, a full block - and is more common for Internet applications.

The performance of symmetric ciphers can vary quite a bit, depending on the key length and the actual algorithm chosen.

With stream ciphers, encryption and decryption can be a simple bit addition modulo 2, which is simply XOR. For example, if you have a key stream and a plaintext stream , you can simply XOR them together to yield a ciphertext stream . Assuming the recipient has the key stream , they can simply XOR with to recover the plaintext .

We also have synchronous and asynchronous stream ciphers. The security of a stream cipher depends entirely on the key stream , which should be random (such that the probability of getting a particular bit is 0.5, or uniformly random), and must be reproducible by both the sender and the receiver. In synchronous stream ciphers, the key stream depends only on the key (and possibly an initialization vector, or IV). On the other hand, in an asynchronous stream cipher, the key stream also depends on the ciphertext, called “dotted feedback”.

But why is modulo 2 addition - or a XOR operation - a good encryption function? For a perfectly random key stream , each ciphertext output bit has a 50% chance to be 0 or 1, making it a good statistic property for the ciphertext. Additionally, inverting XOR is simple, since it is simply the same operation. But this all comes down to choosing the correct key.
