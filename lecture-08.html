In AES, all operations are performed on 8-bit bytes, where addition, multiplication, and division is performed over the finite field .

The cipher text a plaintext block size of 128 bits, or 16 bytes. The key length may be 16, 24, or 32 bytes; 128, 192, or 256 bits, respectively. In turn, the algorithm is referred to as AES-128, AES-192, or AES-256, depending on the key length.

We begin by introducing the AES matrix data structure, in which the 16-byte input, state array, and output is arranged as a matrix, ordered in column-major order, top to bottom, left to right.

There is also the key expanded over many distinct elements. You take each column, form a 4-byte word, and place it into the expansion algorithm to generate successive keys with it. Now that we’ve covered the basics, what actually happens in a “round” of encryption?

Here’s a full diagram of AES in both directions:

The initial key that is provided is expanded into an array of 44 32-bit words (for AES-128). Notice that this yields 11 16-byte round keys; again, remember that 10 keys are actually used in the rounds, but 1 key is used on the plaintext right before the first round.

In each round, data blocks are processed as a single matrix using substitutions and permutations. Broadly speaking, the cipher can be seen as scrambling the block (the first three stages) followed by XOR encryption with the round keys. Each stage is easily reversible.

Worth noting is that the cipher process begins and ends with an AddRoundKey stage (for both encryption and decryption), as a result of the initial transformation, and the round key addition occurring at the end of each round.

However, note that although the decryption algorithm makes use of the expanded key in reverse order, the decryption algorithm is not strictly identical to the encryption algorithm; there are inverse operations in each round that must be performed for decryption to correctly occur.

Finally, in both encryption and decryption, the final round consists of only three stages; there is no mixing of columns applied in the final round of encryption, nor any “unmixing” applied in the final round of decryption.

With that high level overview, what actually happens in each round?

The four steps represented here are:

  • Substitute bytes: We use an S-box to perform a byte-by-byte substitution of the block with something else
  • Shift rows: A simple fixed permutation (the dot is where a particular byte currently is; the end of the arrow is where it ends up)
  • Mix columns: A substitution that makes use of arithmetic over
  • Add round key: A simple bitwise XOR of the current block with a portion of the expanded key

We perform this “round” times depending on the key length, noting that the column mixing step does not occur in the final round.

How do the byte substitution and “add round key” transformations work?

The byte substitution is simply a fixed function, in which the two hex digits are used to look up a particular value in a matrix, known as an S-box. The result is then placed in the same position of the state matrix. (For decryption, an “inverse S-box” exists.)


For example, if we wanted to substitute the byte A4, we would go the row labeled A and the column labeled 5, yielding an output byte of 06. More generally, the byte xy is mapped to its corresponding entry in the table.

The result of doing all this is that it gives us the cryptographic principles of confusion and diffusion. Because a particular bit of the ciphertext was the result of many different bits in key, it’s difficult to yield the key from knowledge of the ciphertext alone (confusion). In turn, small changes in the key yield drastic changes in the output, hiding the relationship between the ciphertext and the key.

Similarly, with diffusion, a small change in the plaintext has a drastic effect on the output; any particular plaintext bit/byte is equally likely to appear as some different plaintext bit/byte in the ciphertext. Any patterns in the plaintext, such as successive bits, would not be apparent in the ciphertext. This is achieved by “diffusing” the information about these patterns in the plaintext across the rows and columns of the cipher.

The S-box was significantly designed to be resistant to known cryptanalytic attacks, achieving diffusion through a nonlinear function. More generally, the developers of Rijndael wanted to find a design where the input bits and outputs have a low correlation, and the output is not some linear mathematical function of the input.
